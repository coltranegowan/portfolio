Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Coltrane Gowan — cgowan@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Use-of-force complaints have disproportionately targeted younger individuals.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Bin Sizes: -1.5

    My choice to make the bins different sizes was made to exaggerate the already present trend of excessive force complaints being higher for complainants under 30. Originally I had uniform bin sizes which did show this trend to an extent, but I decided that making the bins for older ages shorter would make it seem like age was even more of a factor in this data. I think the very obvious negative correlation after the 18-24 bin makes it veyr obvious to the reader of this trend, leaving less room for doubt on smaller details such as the sixe of the bins, since the width of the bars are all the same.

  • Color Choices: -1

    I used 'px.colors.sequential.Reds[::-1]' to create an effect in this graph where the bars got less red as the ages got larger, which was another way to coaxe the reader into the outcome that I wanted them to get from this visualization. The darker red on the left corresponding to younger ages denotes a feeling of more dense, more important data and draws eyes to itself. When a reader's eyes are drawn here, the color plus the height of the bar will make it very easy to draw the conclusion that younger people are more effected by these complaints. The red also draws parallels to blood and violence, which can bring in an empathetic element when talking about violent complaints.

    • Title: -1.5

    One of the main parts of a visualization is the title, and this one invokes even more emotions in the reader. Word choices like 'suffer' helps summon more negative emotions before one even looks at the graph, and a good title can have someone making up their mind before even processing the visualization. My word choice of 'Violations' instead of 'Complaints' also helps this data seem more like actual wrongdoing that occured instead of just a complaint by a citizen.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Color Choice: -1

    The color palette used here again leverages a reversed red scale (px.colors.sequential.Reds[::-1]), where younger age groups are assigned darker reds, and older ones get lighter shades. While this isn’t inherently misleading, it visually links youth with urgency and intensity. In the context of police complaints, red evokes notions of danger or wrongdoing, subtly guiding viewers to focus on the youth. I considered using a neutral or diverging palette (e.g., blues or grays), but the red scale makes the disparity between groups feel more alarming, even when the counts may not be extremely far apart. This subtly pushes a more emotionally charged narrative.

  • Title: -1.5

    The title “The Kids That Cried Wolf: Younger People in New York File More Police Complaints” heavily frames the data with a critical lens. It evokes a well-known moral fable associated with dishonesty and overreaction, implying that these complaints may lack validity. While the data does show more complaints by younger people, the framing undermines their legitimacy and credibility. I chose this phrasing to intentionally provoke skepticism in the reader. A more neutral alternative could have been “Police Complaint Volume by Age Group,” but I opted for this in order to suggest over-reporting in youth, setting up bias before data is even read. I also used misleading bin titles but labeling the first bin 18-24, when that bin includes all ages 0-24. By doing this, I made that bin seem even smaller and more important.

  • Scaling Values (Exaggeration): -2

    The most overtly deceptive choice was applying a power transform (scaled_count = complaint_count ** 1.5) to the y-values. This decision artificially inflates the height differences between age groups, making it seem as if the 18–24 group files exponentially more complaints than others. I chose this to visually overemphasize a real trend—yes, younger groups file more complaints, but this transform makes it look extreme. Linear scaling showed less dramatic differences. While it persuades more effectively, it crosses the line into misleading territory since no axis annotation hints at non-linearity.

Final Reflection

Creating both visualizations challenged me to think deeper about how data design influences perception. For the persuasive side, it was relatively straightforward to guide interpretation by making earnest design choices, like emphasizing trends through clear binning or using emotionally charged color schemes and titles. These choices leaned into the data’s natural story while still maintaining its integrity. On the other hand, designing the deceptive visualization required more intentional manipulation. I had to consciously push boundaries by exaggerating scaling, skewing color emphasis, and crafting a provocative title that cast doubt on the validity of the complaints.

This project reshaped my understanding of what ethical data visualization entails. Ethical analysis is not just about showing the “truth” but doing so in a way that avoids distorting the viewer’s interpretation through visual tricks. It means preserving proportional relationships, labeling clearly, and resisting the urge to editorialize with misleading framing or exaggerated encodings. While persuasion can be acceptable—especially in advocacy or storytelling—there is a line where it turns into manipulation. That line is crossed when design choices intentionally obscure the data’s real structure, provoke unearned emotional reactions, or imply conclusions that the data does not support. From this experience, I see how ethical visualization is a balancing act between narrative clarity and visual honesty.
